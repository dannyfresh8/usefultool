How to Calculate Loan Interest

Learn how to calculate loan interest with our simple guide. Understand simple vs compound interest, APR, and use our free loan calculator.

simple interest formula, compound interest formula, how to calculate APR, monthly loan payment formula, amortization explained

Simple Interest vs Compound Interest

Understanding the difference between simple and compound interest is crucial when borrowing money. Simple interest is calculated only on the principal amount, while compound interest is calculated on the principal plus accumulated interest. Most personal loans use simple interest, while credit cards and investment accounts use compound interest.

The formula for simple interest is Principal × Rate × Time. For example, a $10,000 loan at 5% annual simple interest for 3 years costs $10,000 × 0.05 × 3 = $1,500 in interest.

Compound interest uses the formula A = P(1 + r/n)^(nt) where P is principal, r is the annual rate, n is how often interest compounds, and t is time in years.

How to Calculate Monthly Loan Payments

The standard formula for calculating monthly loan payments is: M = P × [r(1+r)^n] / [(1+r)^n - 1]

Where M = monthly payment, P = principal, r = monthly interest rate (annual rate ÷ 12), and n = total number of payments.

Understanding APR vs Interest Rate

APR (Annual Percentage Rate) includes both the interest rate AND any fees charged by the lender. This makes APR a more accurate measure of what you'll actually pay.

For example, a loan with a 5% interest rate and 2% in fees might have a 6.5% APR. Always compare APRs when shopping for loans, not just the interest rate. Check NerdWallet to compare current rates.

Factors That Affect Your Loan Interest Rate

Your interest rate depends on several factors: credit score (higher score = lower rate), loan amount, loan term, debt-to-income ratio, and current market rates. Improving your credit score by even 50 points can save you thousands over the life of a loan.

Amortization Explained

Amortization is the process of spreading out a loan into equal payments over time. Early payments mostly go toward interest, while later payments go mostly toward principal. Our amortization calculator shows you the full breakdown for any loan.
